STEP 1: MESHING THE MESHES?
------
First, I needed to find the meshes I wanted. I was on the hunt for a pickup truck STL and a VW bus STL.
Finding a pickup truck was easy, but finding a VW bus (that was 1 singular STL) was much harder than I thought.
Here are the STLs I ended up finding:
Chevy Silverado STL: https://www.thingiverse.com/thing:4931055
VW Bus: https://www.thingiverse.com/thing:512454

STEP 2: SLICING & FIXING
------
Next, it was time to slice the cars so that I could separate the pieces I wanted. This meant slicing off the front of the pickup truck and the back of the VW bus.
First, I used < Mesh > to turn them into meshes. Next, I used < QuadRemesh > to create the quad mesh and then < MeshSplit > to split the cars in half using a rectangle polygon. This left me with two open meshes.

First, I used < Check > to check the mesh and < ShowEdges > to find the naked edges.
Closing the pickup truck mesh was pretty easy. I just used < FillMeshHole >. The VW bus was much, MUCH harder!!
It looks like I downloaded a bad mesh and there were a lot of problems. I had to use < DeleteMeshFaces > to delete some parts of the mesh and fill them back in.


STEP 3: TIME TO COMBINE!
------
Finally, I was able to fix the meshes and I used < BooleanUnion > to combine the meshes into this VW pickup truck.
It had no naked edges, non-manifold edges, nor degenerate surfaces...but I couldn't seem to get rid of the "1 extremely short edge."
I decided to render and export it anyways, and added wheels to the front because the original STL didn't have them.


STEP 4: CURA TEST

------------------------ LAMPS PART 1 -------------------------------------------


STEP 1: INSPIRATION & PLANNING
------
Because this assignment involved a light component, I wanted to do something with organic shapes. I found these cool cell-looking things really interesting, and I had seen them in some buildings online.
Later I learned that these are called Voronoi Diagrams, which are "is a partition of a plane into regions close to each of a given set of objects" according to Wikipedia.
I also learned this can be done through parametric modeling... (yes, Grasshopper. My nemesis)
As for the light fixture, I was really inspired by these cascade spiral lamps and wanted to do something similar.


While I still plan to use the lamp innards that were required for the assignment, I decided to try and bring another light component into the mix.
I had the idea of creating a bunch of Voronoi spheres, and adding an LED candle inside the spheres to create a cool lighting effect.
I then wanted to assemble everything to hang like the cascade lamps I showed earlier, using the lamp innard as a support.

STEP 2: RHINO, GRASSHOPPER, STRUGGLE
------
I was completely new to voronoi spheres (and still a Grasshopper noob) so I did some reading and watched a tutorial on how to create voronoi spheres.
I began with a sphere with a radius of 25, and for the random points for the voronoi, I used the "Populate Geometry" component, with a count of 0-100.
I also used a "Bounding Box" component to give the voronoi shapes a boundary. With the "Scale" component, I was able to create a smaller sphere within the sphere, which helped give the wall of the sphere a certain width.


I used an "extrude" component at the end to extrude the voronoi pattern. And then it was time to bake!
When baking, I learned that you need to check off the box for "Group" before baking (I will show what happens when you don't later)

STEP 3: SLICING & PRINTING!
------
The fist time I imported into Cura, I could immediately tell something went wrong because I had the "extraneous surfaces" error (sphere on the right).
I think this could have been because I didn't group before baking, or I did not extrude properly before baking.
I had to redo the process, and the second time it imported with no issues! (See sphere on the left).


For this print, I thought it might be smart to use "tree branch" supports, beacuse it's such a complex shape.
I thought the branches could reach in through the nooks and crannies and help support the top of the sphere, which I was worried would cave in.
Below you can see a preview of the print, with a top view to show the tree branches winding into the inside of the sphere.


Lastly, in order to pause the print halfway and add my candle, I had to add a Post Processing Script - basically, it's a command that interrupts the G code to tell the printer to pause.
I asked the printer to pause after finishing layer #218, which based on the preview I guesstimated would be tell enough to hold my candle.
I also told the nozzle to move out of the way (to coordinate 0, 230) and maintain a standby temp of 180 so that I would be able to add the candle in.
this took quite a few tries but I figured it out!
